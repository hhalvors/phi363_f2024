Religion and Scientific Objectivity

H Halvorson and Z Quanbeck

Summary: This course investigates the concept of “objectivity”, with special reference to science and religion, and both from historical and conceptual points of view.

There have been moments in history when some or other thinker believes himself to have found “the recipe” for achieving objectivity – a kind of epistemic apotheosis. Without fail, these recipes have proved to be self-destructive, i.e. causing individual thinkers to be less objective than they might have been otherwise. Take, for example, the dialectical method of G.W.F. Hegel, the bad outcomes of which were pointed out most colorfully by Kierkegaard. Or consider the formal methods of the logical positivists, which similarly failed to provide an objective framework for scientific thinking.

We will also consider the scope of objectivity in the social sciences, as well as the relationship between objectivity and values in accepting scientific theories, making religious commitments, and forming beliefs in other domains.

Format: This course meets twice a week for 80 minutes and is discussion-based.
